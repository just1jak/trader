## Book Outline + AI Image Generation Plan

## 1. Book Structure & Outline

**Target Audience:** Ages 6–10 (grades 1–4)  
**Format:** 20–24 illustrated pages  
**Total Word Count:** 2,000–3,000 words  
**Tone:** Playful, curious, discovery-based learning; Roo as a relatable guide asking questions and exploring circuits with wonder

### Chapter Breakdown

#### **Chapter 1: What's a Circuit? (Pages 1–3)**
*Word count: ~250 words*

Meet Roo, a curious rabbit engineer who loves building things! Roo discovers a mysterious glowing lamp at home and wonders: "How does this light work?" Through simple dialogue, we introduce the concept of a circuit as a "path for electricity to travel." Roo learns that circuits need three things: a power source (battery), a path (wires), and a consumer (light bulb). Illustrations show Roo examining the lamp, tracing the wire path, and discovering a battery. The chapter ends with Roo eager to build her own circuit.

**Key concepts:** Circuit definition, power source, conductor, electrical path  
**Vocabulary:** Battery, wire, bulb, electricity, circuit

#### **Chapter 2: Roo Meets the LED (Pages 4–7)**
*Word count: ~600 words*

Roo gathers supplies: a battery, LED, and wires. With a friend (Squirrel Sam), Roo builds her first circuit—a simple series circuit with an LED that lights up! Roo learns about polarity ("the LED has two ends, and one direction makes it glow!") and the importance of the complete path. Through trial-and-error play, Roo discovers what happens when the circuit breaks (light goes out) and what happens when she reconnects it (light returns). The section celebrates the magic of "lighting up" and Roo's growing confidence as a circuit builder.

**Key concepts:** Series circuit, LED polarity, complete circuit, open vs. closed circuit  
**Vocabulary:** Polarity, positive/negative, LED, anode/cathode (simplified), complete circuit

#### **Chapter 3: Roo Adds a Switch (Pages 8–11)**
*Word count: ~500 words*

Roo realizes: "What if I could turn the light on and off without breaking the wires?" Enter the switch! Roo learns that a switch is a controllable break in the circuit. By pressing a button, Roo opens and closes the path, controlling power flow. Illustrations show the switch mechanism (a simple contact point) and Roo experimenting with different switch positions. Roo decorates her circuit with a colorful cardboard house around the switch, turning it into a "control center." The chapter emphasizes user control and the fun of interactive building.

**Key concepts:** Switches, open/closed circuits, circuit control, mechanical switches  
**Vocabulary:** Switch, contact, control, open circuit, closed circuit

#### **Chapter 4: Series vs. Parallel (Pages 12–18)**
*Word count: ~800 words*

Roo wants to light up *two* LEDs! She tries connecting them one after another (series) and notices something: both light up, but dimmer than before. Why? Roo learns about voltage drop and current sharing. Then, Roo tries a different arrangement (parallel)—both LEDs stay bright! Roo discovers that parallel circuits divide the path, allowing independent power flow to each LED. Through colorful diagrams and Roo's "Aha!" moments, children learn the difference:

- **Series:** LEDs share power, brightness decreases  
- **Parallel:** LEDs get independent power, brightness stays high

Roo builds both circuits and compares brightness, learning real engineering trade-offs.

**Key concepts:** Series circuit, parallel circuit, voltage, current, brightness comparison  
**Vocabulary:** Series, parallel, voltage drop, current, brightness, efficiency

#### **Chapter 5: Roo's Challenge (Pages 19–24)**
*Word count: ~450 words*

Roo faces a mission: "Build a circuit with *three* LEDs, a switch, and keep all three bright!" This chapter recaps everything—Roo must decide: series or parallel? She sketches designs, tests them, and learns that parallel is better for three LEDs. Roo also discovers she can combine series *and* parallel for creative configurations. The finale celebrates Roo's achievement with a full-page circuit diagram showing her successful build. The ending is open-ended: "What will you build next with circuits?"—encouraging children to experiment at home.

**Key concepts:** Circuit design, problem-solving, series + parallel combination  
**Vocabulary:** Breadboard (optional), circuit diagram, design process

## 2. AI Image Generation Plan

### Tool Recommendation

**Primary Tool: DALL-E 3** (via OpenAI API)
- **Cost:** ~$0.04–0.08 per image (1,024×1,024 px) = **$1–2 per image**
- **Total budget:** ~$20–48 for all 20–24 images
- **Turnaround:** 5–10 seconds per image; all 24 images in <10 minutes
- **Strengths:** Text adherence, consistent quality, integration-friendly, fast iteration
- **Drawback:** Style consistency requires careful prompting

**Alternative: Midjourney**
- **Cost:** $20/mo subscription (unlimited images after cap)
- **Quality:** Excellent, artistic consistency
- **Turnaround:** ~30 sec–2 min per image (queue dependent)
- **Best if:** Willing to pay upfront; aesthetic priority over cost

**Alternative: Runway Gen-3**
- **Cost:** ~$100/mo (video + image generation)
- **Overkill for static book; not recommended**

**Recommendation:** **Start with DALL-E 3** for cost efficiency and speed; upgrade to Midjourney if user prefers artistic style over budget.

### Image Count & Specifications

- **Total images:** 20–24 (one per page, with 2–3 full-page spreads)
- **Dimensions:** 1,024×1,024 px (square); can be cropped/resized for layout
- **Style:** Consistent children's book illustration aesthetic
- **Art direction:** Warm, colorful, friendly; flat or soft 3D; bright backgrounds
