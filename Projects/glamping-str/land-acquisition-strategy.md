# Land Acquisition Strategy for Adjacent Parcels
## Santa Cruz Mountains Glamping STR Expansion

## Methods to Find Adjacent Land Owners

### 1. County Assessor/Auditor-Controller Office (Primary Source)
- **Santa Cruz County Assessor's Office**: https://www.santacruzcounty.us/assessor/
- **Online Parcel Search**: Most counties have GIS mapping tools where you can:
  - Enter your APN to view your parcel
  - Use "identify" or "buffer" tools to see surrounding parcels
  - Click on adjacent parcels to get owner names and mailing addresses
- **In-Person Visit**: Assessor's office staff can help with parcel maps and ownership details

### 2. GIS Mapping Platforms
- **Santa Cruz County GIS Portal**: Look for parcel viewer or mapping applications
- **ParcelQuest**: Subscription service with detailed parcel data (parcelquest.com)
- **LandVision** or **Regrid**: Mobile/apps for parcel information
- **Google Earth Pro**: Shows parcel boundaries in some areas when enabled

### 3. Title Company Resources
- Request a **preliminary title report** for your property - it often shows adjacent owners
- Title companies can run **ownership encumbrance reports** for surrounding parcels
- Build relationship with a local title rep who can pull data efficiently

### 4. Real Estate Professional Assistance
- Local real estate agents have MLS access and can run comparative market analyses
- Agents can set up automated alerts for when adjacent properties come on market
- Some specialize in land transactions and rural properties

### 5. Public Records Research
- **Santa Cruz County Recorder**: Tracks deeds, liens, and transfers
- **Assessor-Controller**: Handles tax bills and delinquency lists
- **Planning Department**: Has records of permits, variances, and subdivision applications

### 6. Direct Observation & Outreach
- Drive the area looking for "For Sale" signs (though these may not be up yet)
- Talk to neighbors - they often know who might be considering selling
- Look for signs of neglect or absentee ownership (overgrown, disrepair)

## Checking Auction Status

### 1. Tax Defaulted Property Auctions
- **Santa Cruz County Tax Collector**: Handles tax-defaulted property sales
- Website: https://www.santacruzcounty.us/taxcollector/
- Look for "Tax Defaulted Property" or "Redemption" sections
- Properties typically go to auction after being tax-defaulted for 5+ years
- **Key**: Check the delinquent tax list published annually (usually around June-July)

### 2. Bank Foreclosure Auctions
- **Public Trustee's Office**: Handles trustee sales (foreclosures)
- Check local newspapers for legal notices (Santa Cruz Sentinel)
- Online services: Foreclosure.com, RealtyTrac, Auction.com (may require subscription)
- **Notice of Default (NOD)**: First step in foreclosure process - public record
- **Notice of Trustee Sale**: Sets auction date - also public record

### 3. Probate & Estate Sales
- **Probate Court**: Handles estate properties
- Look for "Notice of Petition to Administer Estate" in legal notices
- Properties may sell through probate auction or private sale

### 4. Government Surplus Land
- Check federal (GSA), state, and local agency surplus property listings
- Less common for private adjacent parcels but worth checking

## Practical Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Initial Research (Week 1)
1. [ ] Get your parcel map from Santa Cruz County Assessor website
2. [ ] Identify APNs of all immediately adjacent properties (N, S, E, W, plus diagonals)
3. [ ] Use assessor's online tool to get owner names and mailing addresses for each
4. [ ] Create spreadsheet tracking: APN, Owner Name, Mailing Address, Property Use (if visible)

### Phase 2: Deeper Investigation (Week 2)
1. [ ] Search recent deed transactions for each APN at County Recorder
2. [ ] Check if any properties show as tax-defaulted on delinquency list
3. [ ] Look for recent mortgages or liens that might indicate financial stress
4. [ ] Research property characteristics: size, zoning, improvements, access

### Phase 3: Outreach Strategy (Week 3-4)
1. [ ] Determine best contact method for each owner:
    - Mail letter (most formal, good for absentee owners)
    - Phone call (if phone number can be found)
    - In-person visit (if local and appropriate)
2. [ ] Prepare professional introduction letter explaining:
    - Your interest in the area
    - That you're a local resident planning responsible development
    - That you're not a developer seeking to flip quickly
    - Open to discussing options (purchase, lease, partnership)
3. [ ] Consider offering to pay for a professional appraisal if they're serious about selling

### Phase 4: Auction Monitoring (Ongoing)
1. [ ] Subscribe to Santa Cruz County Tax Collector auction notifications
2. [ ] Set up Google Alerts for: "[APN] Santa Cruz County", "[Owner Name] estate"
3. [ ] Check trustee sale lists weekly if monitoring foreclosures
4. [ ] Build relationship with local real estate attorney who handles auction purchases

## Key Considerations for Adjacent Land Purchase

### Benefits of Contiguous Ownership
- Ability to expand glamping operation scale
- Improved access and circulation between properties
- Potential for buffer zones (privacy, noise reduction)
- Control over viewshed and immediate surroundings
- Simplified utilities and infrastructure planning

### Valuation Factors to Research
- Comparable land sales in area (price per acre)
- Development potential (zoning, utilities access, building envelopes)
- Water rights and availability
- Timber or mineral rights (if applicable)
- Easements, rights-of-way, or restrictions
- Access quality (road condition, maintenance agreements)

### Approaching Owners
- **Timing**: Avoid approaching during known difficult times (holidays, harvest season if agricultural)
- **Tone**: Emphasize stewardship and compatibility with area character
- **Flexibility**: Consider creative options:
    - Purchase with seller financing
    - Lease with option to buy
    - Purchase of development rights only
    - Joint venture or partnership
- **Confidentiality**: Initially, you may not want to reveal full expansion plans

## Risk Mitigation

### Due Diligence Checklist Before Making Offer
1. [ ] Verify exact boundaries with survey (don't rely on maps alone)
2. [ ] Check for easements (utility, access, conservation)
3. [ ] Investigate any code violations or permits needed
4. [ ] Confirm water availability and rights (critical for STR)
5. [ ] Review access rights - especially if using private roads
6. [ ] Check for endangered species or habitat restrictions
7. [ ] Verify zoning allows intended use (or what variances needed)
8. [ ] Investigate any pending assessments or special tax districts

### Communication Best Practices
- Always identify yourself clearly and honestly
- Respect privacy - don't trespass to take photos or survey
- Keep records of all communications
- Consider having initial contact come through a neutral third party (attorney or agent) if sensitivity is high
- Be prepared to explain why you want the land and what you plan to do
