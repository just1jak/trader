# SmokeTology: Safe Operating Practices

A practical guide to running SmokeTology legitimately while minimizing IRS audit risk and maintaining tax-exempt integrity.

---

## Core Principle

**Operate as if you'll be audited.** If every decision, donation, and expense can withstand scrutiny, you're safe. The IRS isn't anti-religion — they're anti-abuse.

---

## 1. Financial Separation (Critical)

### Strict Personal/Church Boundary

✅ **Always:**
- Separate bank account for SmokeTology (never mix with personal funds)
- Separate credit/debit card for church expenses only
- Separate accounting records
- Church owns church property; you own personal property
- Receipts for every church transaction

❌ **Never:**
- Pay personal bills from church account
- Use church card for personal purchases
- Deposit church funds into personal account
- "Borrow" from the church account (even short-term)
- Use church vehicles/property for personal use without proper tracking

### Reasonable Reimbursement

If you spend personal money for church purposes:
1. Save receipts
2. Submit to church (with board approval if over $X)
3. Get reimbursed from church account
4. Document in church records

## 2. Donation Documentation

### Every Donation Must Have

For donations over $250:
- Written acknowledgment from SmokeTology
- Date, amount, and statement: "No goods or services were provided in exchange"
- Donor name and address

For donations over $500 (non-cash):
- Form 8283 (Noncash Charitable Contributions)
- Description of property
- Fair market value

For donations over $5,000 (non-cash):
- Independent qualified appraisal
- Form 8283 Section B signed by appraiser

## 3. Board Governance

### Real, Not Paper, Governance

**Board composition:**
- Minimum 3 members (California requirement)
- **At least 1 non-related, non-family member** (critical for legitimacy)
- Board members should not all be employees/relatives of founder

**Regular meetings:**
- Minimum quarterly board meetings
- Written minutes for every meeting
- Document major decisions (spending over $X, new initiatives, etc.)
- Annual meeting required

**Decisions to document:**
- Annual budget approval
- Large expenditures (set a threshold, e.g., $1,000)
- Hiring/compensation decisions
- Property acquisitions
- Mission/ceremony changes

### Board Meeting Minutes Template

## 4. Activity Documentation

### Prove SmokeTology Is Real

Maintain records of:

**Ceremonies & Gatherings:**
- Date, location, attendees (rough count is fine)
- Photos (with consent)
- Description of religious content
- Who led the ceremony

**Religious Activity Log:**
```

Event: Spring Solstice Smoking Ceremony
Location: [Member's property / community space]
Attendees: 12 congregants
Activities: 
  - Opening blessing for the animal
  - 6-hour smoking ceremony
  - Communal sharing of meal
  - Gratitude circle
  - Discussion of seasonal teachings

```

**Recommended cadence:**
- At least monthly gathering (informal counts)
- Major seasonal/holy day ceremonies (4+ per year)
- Educational/study gatherings (optional but helpful)

## 5. Membership Records

### Track Your Congregation

Keep a member roster:
- Names of regular attendees
- Date they joined the community
- Their role/involvement
- Contact info (with consent)

**Why this matters:** "Real church" indicators require demonstrating actual membership. A church of 1 (or only family) is a red flag.

**Membership growth targets:**
- Year 1: 5–10 active members
- Year 2: 10–20 active members
- Year 3+: 20+ active members

These don't need to be huge numbers, but they need to be **real, non-family members**.

## 6. Fund Usage Guidelines

### Acceptable Church Expenses

✅ **Clearly mission-aligned:**
- Meat and supplies for ceremonies
- Wood/equipment for smoking
- Venue rental for gatherings
- Religious literature/materials
- Travel for legitimate ministry purposes
- Educational programs about the faith
- Charitable outreach aligned with mission
- Reasonable administrative costs (phone, supplies, etc.)

### Caution Zone (Need Strong Documentation)

⚠️ **Justifiable but watch carefully:**
- Vehicle use (must log church vs. personal miles)
- Home use as church space (only document church-specific portion)
- Founder compensation (must be reasonable; document board approval)
- Conferences/training (must be ministry-related)

### Prohibited (Will Trigger Issues)

❌ **Don't ever:**
- Pay your personal mortgage/rent from church funds
- Pay your personal groceries (unless explicitly for ceremony use, documented)
- Pay personal vehicle expenses without mileage logs
- Pay family members salaries without legitimate work
- Distribute "profits" to founder/members
- Use church for political campaigns
- Use church to lobby (limited lobbying allowed; campaigning prohibited)

## 7. Founder Compensation (If Any)

### Best Practice: Start Unpaid

For the first 1–2 years, take **zero compensation**:
- Demonstrates legitimacy
- Avoids "private inurement" concerns
- You support yourself from for-profit DBA/LLC

### If You Eventually Take a Salary

Must be:
- **Reasonable** (comparable to similar-sized churches)
- **Approved by board** (with vote in minutes)
- **Documented** in W-2 or 1099
- **Taxed appropriately** (you pay income tax on it)
- **Disclosed** if you ever file Form 990

**Reasonable salary benchmarks:**
- Small church (<50 members): $20k–40k/year part-time
- Medium church (50–200): $40k–70k/year
- Document comparable salaries from similar orgs

## 8. Personal Donation Strategy

### Safe Donation Levels

Based on your income:

| Annual Income | Safe Donation | Caution Zone | Red Zone |
|--------------|---------------|--------------|----------|
| $50,000 | $0–5,000 | $5,000–10,000 | $10,000+ |
| $100,000 | $0–10,000 | $10,000–20,000 | $20,000+ |
| $200,000 | $0–20,000 | $20,000–40,000 | $40,000+ |

**Rule of thumb:** Stay under 10% of AGI for donations to your own church. Above 20% is a red flag.

### Donation Best Practices

1. Don't max out your AGI deduction limit
2. Spread donations throughout the year (not one big year-end check)
3. Always get acknowledgment letters from the church
4. Pay via check or transfer (paper trail), not cash
5. Match donations to demonstrable church needs/activities

## 9. Annual Review Checklist

Every year, verify:

- [ ] Board met at least 4 times with documented minutes
- [ ] Bank accounts remain separate from personal
- [ ] All major expenses have receipts
- [ ] Donation acknowledgment letters sent to all donors
- [ ] At least one non-family board member active
- [ ] Religious activities documented (10+ events minimum)
- [ ] Member roster updated
- [ ] Personal donations under 20% of AGI
- [ ] If applicable: Form 990-N filed (if revenue $50k+, Form 990-EZ)
- [ ] Statement of Information filed with CA SOS (biennial)

## 11. Red Flags to Avoid

🚨 **Things that will trigger scrutiny:**

1. **Founder lives off church** — Using donations as personal income
2. **No real congregation** — Only you and family members
3. **No real activity** — Just paperwork, no actual ceremonies
4. **Donation = tax savings** — Personal donations exactly match your tax liability reduction
5. **Property entanglement** — Church "owns" your house/car
6. **Round number donations** — Suspicious if no clear basis
7. **Family on payroll** — Without legitimate work performed
8. **Mission drift** — Spending on things unrelated to stated purpose
9. **No board independence** — Spouse + 2 family members as the only board
10. **Inflated property valuations** — Donating "art" or property at inflated values

## 12. When to Consult Professionals

Hire a professional when:

- **CPA**: Annual taxes, fund usage questions, donation strategy
- **Non-profit attorney**: Bylaws review, 501(c)(3) application, audit response
- **Religious org consultant**: Best practices, governance issues

**Estimated costs:**
- CPA review: $500–1,500/year
- Attorney consultation: $200–400/hour
- 501(c)(3) preparation: $1,500–3,500

Worth the investment once revenue exceeds $25k/year.

## Summary: The "Sleep Well at Night" Test

Before any decision, ask:

1. **Is this clearly mission-aligned?** (If no → don't do it)
2. **Would I be comfortable explaining this to an IRS auditor?** (If no → don't do it)
3. **Is it documented?** (If no → document it before proceeding)
4. **Does it benefit the community, not just me personally?** (If only personal benefit → reconsider)

If you can answer "yes" to all four, you're operating safely.
